Recovery Methods

Evidence-based recovery modalities from EPI Phase 2. Adequate recovery restores physiological and psychological processes so athletes can train or compete at the required level. Mechanical stress (sprinting, lifting) and metabolic stress (endurance) require different recovery approaches.

Hydrotherapy

Cold Water Immersion (CWI)

Strong Evidence
  • Protocol: 1 × 5 min @ 10°C (50°F)
  • Immersion depth: waist to shoulder
  • Reduces DOMS, speeds muscle function recovery
  • Outperforms hot water immersion and passive recovery for sprint and time trial performance
Hydrotherapy

Contrast Water Therapy (CWT)

Strong Evidence
  • Protocol: 4 min @ 40°C + 2 min @ 10°C × 2 rounds (always finish cold)
  • Alternates hot and cold immersion to create "pump" effect
  • Comparable to CWI for performance recovery
  • Most popular in elite sports settings
Active Recovery

Low-Intensity Active Recovery

Moderate Evidence
  • Protocol: Stationary bike 1 × 15 min @ 50–60% MaxHR
  • Most common recovery method used by athletes
  • Role in lactate clearance — evidence incomplete but anecdotally effective
  • No detrimental effects vs. passive recovery
Compression

Compression Garments

Moderate Evidence
  • Protocol: Wear immediately after training for remainder of day
  • Improves venous return via graduated compression (proximal → distal)
  • Moderate benefit: reduces muscle soreness and CK levels
  • Improves recovery of muscle strength and power
Manual Therapy

Massage

Limited Performance Evidence
  • Beneficial for psychological recovery and perceived soreness
  • Limited evidence for improving functional performance recovery
  • Valuable for injury prevention — improves muscle tone and resting length
  • Should be included in programs for reasons beyond recovery
Flexibility

Stretching

Inconclusive Evidence
  • Research is mixed — not conclusively effective as recovery modality
  • Some studies show improved power output 15 hrs post-match
  • Others show no benefit vs. CWT or active recovery
  • Implement case-by-case — if client feels it helps, allow it
Ranked by evidence for performance recovery:
  • 1. CWI / CWT (Hydrotherapy): Strongest evidence for reducing DOMS and improving next-day performance. CWT slightly preferred by athletes. Use post-high-intensity or competition days.
  • 2. Active Recovery: Most widely used. No harm, some benefit. Best for metabolic stress recovery (high-intensity cardio/conditioning).
  • 3. Compression Garments: Moderate evidence. Practical and passive — good for travel or post-session without access to pools.
  • 4. Massage: Psychological benefit is real. Physical performance benefit is limited. Still valuable for muscle tone and injury prevention.
  • 5. Stretching: Trainer and athlete preference. Implement case-by-case. Not harmful. May have minor post-match benefit.
Sleep — The Overlooked Priority Sleep is the most powerful recovery intervention and often the most neglected. 7–9 hours of quality sleep per night is the foundation all other modalities build on. Prioritize sleep education with every client before implementing recovery modalities.

Strength & Power

EPI Phase 2 Strength & Power content — neuromuscular system, hypertrophy mechanisms, force-velocity curve, and training methods. Strength (Force = Mass × Acceleration) is the prerequisite for Power (Force × Velocity).

Neuromuscular

Motor Unit Recruitment

  • Size Principle: CNS recruits smallest (Type I) units first, progresses to larger (Type II) as force demands increase
  • Jogging = low threshold Type I recruitment; Sprinting = high threshold Type II
  • Two factors determining muscle recruitment: level of force and rate of force development (RFD)
  • Lifting lighter loads fast recruits most motor units at loads as low as 33% 1RM
Neuromuscular

Rate Coding & Synchronisation

  • Rate Coding: Increasing frequency of neural impulses to already-recruited motor units — force increases without additional recruitment
  • Explosive exercises (Olympic lifts) increase rate coding of higher-threshold motor units
  • Motor Unit Synchronisation: Simultaneous activation of 2+ motor units — more related to RFD
  • Heavy strength training may increase synchronisation, contributing to force production
Muscle Fiber Types

Type I vs Type II

  • Type I (Slow Twitch): Fatigue resistant, peak tension ~110ms, oxidative fuel, endurance
  • Type II (Fast Twitch): Peak tension ~50ms, glycolytic, strength and power, fatigues quickly
  • Type IIa = intermediate; Type IIx = most powerful, most fatigable
  • Average human: ~equal Type I and II, with large interindividual variability
Hypertrophy

3 Mechanisms of Muscle Growth

  • 1. Mechanical Tension: Primary driver — load applied to muscle stimulates mTOR signalling and protein synthesis
  • 2. Metabolic Stress: "The pump" — lactate/H+ accumulation causes cell swelling, hormonal response, anabolic signalling
  • 3. Muscle Damage (EIMD): Eccentric actions cause most damage — structural changes drive adaptation and hypertrophy
Hypertrophy Techniques

Advanced Training Methods

  • Cluster Sets: Short intraset rests (10–30s) between mini-reps — better mechanical tension, less metabolic stress. Best for functional hypertrophy.
  • Supersets: Two exercises back-to-back. Can target same or opposing muscle groups. Increases metabolic stress + volume.
  • Drop Sets: Reduce load ~10% immediately at fatigue and continue. High metabolic stress.
  • Pre-Exhaustion: Isolation exercise first to pre-fatigue agonist before compound lift. Maximizes metabolic stress on target muscle.
Power Development

Complex Training & PAP

  • Complex Training: Pair heavy loaded exercise with biomechanically similar plyometric exercise in same set
  • Post-Activation Potentiation (PAP): Prior heavy contraction enhances explosive capability — neural excitability + myosin phosphorylation
  • Intracomplex rest: 1–4 minutes between loaded and plyometric exercise
  • Requires: >2 years RT, >1.8 relative 1RM lower body, >1.4 relative 1RM upper body
  • Example: Back Squat 3×3 @ 90% → (rest 1–2 min) → CMJ 3×4

Injury Modification Guide

Exercise modifications for common client complaints. Trainers modify — not diagnose. Always advise clients to consult a physician or PT for diagnosis.

Scope of practice: Stop training and advise physician/PT consult if client reports sharp, radiating, sudden-onset, or worsening pain.
Lower Body

Knee Pain / Anterior Knee

AvoidDeep knee flexion past 90°, plyometrics, forward-knee lunges

Modify To
  • Box squat (controlled depth, sit back)
  • Leg press (foot position high)
  • Seated leg extension (pain-free arc)
  • Step-ups (6–8 inch box)
  • Romanian deadlift / hip hinge
  • Lying or seated hamstring curls
Lower Body

Low Back Pain

AvoidLoaded spinal flexion, heavy deadlifts, sit-ups, good mornings

Modify To
  • Bird dog (core anti-extension)
  • Dead bug progressions
  • Glute bridges / hip thrusts
  • Pallof press (anti-rotation)
  • Cable pull-throughs
  • Trap bar deadlift if pain-free
Upper Body

Shoulder Impingement

AvoidOverhead press, upright rows, behind-neck movements, internal rotation under load

Modify To
  • Landmine press
  • Cable face pulls
  • Band external rotation
  • Neutral-grip DB press
  • Seated cable row (horizontal)
  • Scapular pushups
Lower Body

Plantar Fasciitis

AvoidRunning, jumping, high-impact cardio, prolonged standing on hard surfaces

Modify To
  • Seated stationary bike
  • Swimming / aqua walking
  • Upper body or seated resistance
  • Foot rolling pre-session
  • Calf stretching 3×30s each session
Upper Body

Tennis / Golfer's Elbow

AvoidBarbell curls supinated, heavy gripping, wrist curls

Modify To
  • Neutral/hammer curls
  • TRX rows (reduced grip)
  • Machine-based pulling
  • Wrist flexor/extensor stretches
  • Eccentric wrist exercises

Hip Flexor Strain

AvoidSprinting, high-knee drills, hanging leg raises

Modify To
  • Glute bridges (hip extension focus)
  • Prone hip extensions
  • Side-lying clamshells
  • Short-stride walking lunges

The Foundation

Why Nutrition is 80 Percent

Training creates the stimulus. Nutrition provides the raw materials. Without adequate protein, calories, and micronutrients, training adaptations are severely blunted. A client can train perfectly and eat poorly and make minimal progress. The reverse — eating well with imperfect training — yields far better results.

Trainer Role

What You Can Do

  • Educate on calorie awareness and energy balance
  • Emphasize protein priority for body composition
  • Provide general nutrient timing guidance
  • Recommend evidence-based supplements
  • Refer to RD for clinical or therapeutic nutrition
The Single Most Impactful Variable

Protein First

Protein is the most impactful single nutrition variable for body composition. It preserves lean mass in a deficit, drives muscle protein synthesis in a surplus, has the highest thermic effect of any macronutrient (25-30%), and increases satiety. Every client, every goal — protein first.

Fat Loss Nutrition

Evidence-based strategies for maximizing fat loss while preserving lean muscle mass.

The Fundamentals

Energy Balance

  • Deficit: 300-500 kcal below TDEE for sustainable loss
  • Rate of loss: 0.5-1% of body weight per week
  • Faster loss = more muscle loss
  • Slower loss = better muscle retention
  • Track weekly average weight — not daily fluctuations
Protein Priority

Preserve Muscle in a Deficit

  • Target: 1.6-2.2 g/kg body weight
  • Higher end (2.2 g/kg) during aggressive cuts
  • Distribute 30-40g per meal for maximum MPS
  • Protein has highest thermic effect — 25-30% of calories burned in digestion
  • Most satiating macronutrient — reduces hunger
Common Mistakes

Why Clients Stall

  • Not tracking accurately — liquid calories, cooking oils, condiments
  • Eating too little — metabolic adaptation and muscle loss
  • Weekend overeating wiping out weekday deficit
  • Cardio compensation — eating back exercise calories
  • Scale obsession — water retention masks fat loss
Trainer Script

When Client Says "I Am Eating Nothing"

"The scale not moving is not always about eating less — sometimes it is about eating more strategically. Let us look at protein first, then overall calories. Often clients are under-eating protein and over-eating carbs and fat without realizing it."

Muscle Building Nutrition

Nutritional strategies for maximizing hypertrophy and lean mass gain.

Caloric Surplus

Lean Bulk Protocol

  • Surplus: +200-400 kcal above TDEE
  • Rate of gain: 0.25-0.5 lb/week for advanced, 0.5-1 lb/week for beginners
  • Exceeding 1 lb/week = excessive fat gain
  • Track monthly — adjust surplus if gaining too fast
Protein

Muscle Protein Synthesis

  • Target: 1.6-2.2 g/kg body weight
  • Leucine threshold: minimum 2-3g leucine per meal to trigger MPS
  • Distribute evenly — 4-5 meals with 30-40g protein each
  • Pre-sleep casein: 30-40g before bed extends overnight MPS
Carbohydrates

The Muscle Building Fuel

  • Target: 5-7 g/kg body weight
  • Carbs spare protein from being used as fuel
  • Prioritize carbs around training window
  • Do not fear carbs during a building phase
  • Glycogen replenishment drives performance and recovery
Timing

Nutrient Timing for Growth

  • Pre-workout: 30-50g carbs + 20-30g protein 1-2 hrs before
  • Post-workout: 30-50g protein + 40-80g carbs within 2 hrs
  • The anabolic window is wider than once thought — total daily intake matters most
  • Consistent meal timing supports hormonal rhythms

GLP-1 / AOM Nutrition

The most critical nutrition knowledge for trainers right now. Without the right nutritional approach, clients on GLP-1 medications lose significant muscle mass alongside fat.

The Core Problem

25-40% of Weight Lost is Muscle

Without adequate protein intake and resistance training, research shows that 25-40% of weight lost on GLP-1 medications is lean muscle mass — not fat. This is the most important thing your GLP-1 clients need to understand. Protein and resistance training are the intervention.

Protein Targets

Higher Than Standard

  • Target: 1.8-2.4 g/kg body weight
  • Higher than standard due to appetite suppression and anabolic resistance
  • Protein-first eating at every meal — eat protein before anything else
  • Distribute across 4-5 small meals — large meals often not tolerable
  • Protein shakes frequently necessary to hit daily targets
Practical Challenges

What GLP-1 Clients Actually Experience

  • Severe appetite suppression — may feel full after 3-4 bites
  • Nausea — especially in first weeks of dose increases
  • Food aversions — previously enjoyed foods may become unappealing
  • Very low calorie intake — often 800-1200 kcal/day
  • Fatigue — low energy affecting training capacity
Trainer Actions

What You Do Every Session

  • Reinforce protein targets at every session — make it a habit check-in
  • Recommend protein shakes when food intake is too low
  • Adjust training intensity on high nausea or low energy days
  • Limit cardio to 2-3 days/week — excessive cardio accelerates muscle loss
  • Refer to RD for individualized meal planning
When Client Says They Cannot Eat Enough "I understand the medication is making it hard to eat. The most important thing we can do right now is make sure almost every calorie you do eat is protein. Even if you can only eat 1000 calories today, we want at least 100-120g of that to be protein. A protein shake in the morning and one in the afternoon gets you halfway there." When Client Skips Meals Due to Nausea "On nausea days, go liquid. A protein shake with water or milk, a Greek yogurt, or a small amount of cottage cheese — these are easy on the stomach and still give you the protein your muscles need." Protein-First Eating Strategy Teach clients to eat protein first at every meal before any other food. Since the medication causes early satiety, this ensures protein is consumed before appetite is exhausted.

Older Adult Nutrition (55+)

Nutritional considerations specific to clients over 55. Anabolic resistance means older adults need MORE protein than younger adults to achieve the same muscle protein synthesis response.

Anabolic Resistance

Why Older Adults Need More Protein

Anabolic resistance is the reduced sensitivity of muscle protein synthesis to both amino acids and insulin in older adults. The leucine threshold — the minimum amount of leucine needed to trigger MPS — is higher. This means older adults need more protein per meal to get the same muscle-building signal as younger adults.

Protein Targets

55+ Protein Recommendations

  • Target: 1.6-2.0 g/kg body weight
  • Minimum 30-40g protein per meal to overcome anabolic resistance
  • Leucine-rich sources: eggs, dairy, poultry, fish, red meat
  • Distribute evenly across 3-4 meals
  • Do not skip breakfast protein
Key Micronutrients

Critical for 55+

  • Calcium: 1200 mg/day (women 51+), 1000 mg (men 51+)
  • Vitamin D: 800-2000 IU/day — most are deficient
  • B12: Absorption decreases with age — supplementation often necessary
  • Magnesium: 300-400 mg/day — sleep and muscle function
  • Omega-3: 1-3g/day — anti-inflammatory and joint support
Hydration Alert

Diminished Thirst Response

Older adults experience a reduced thirst sensation — they may be significantly dehydrated before feeling thirsty. Encourage consistent water intake throughout the day, not just during exercise. Dehydration in older adults impairs cognition, balance, and exercise performance more than in younger adults.

Weight Cycling and Yo-Yo Dieting

Why It Happens

The Biology of Rebound

Breaking the Cycle

Rest Day Nutrition

The Principle

Common Mistake

The Rest Day Binge

Many clients unconsciously eat more on rest days — boredom eating, social eating, or feeling like they "earned" it. This single behavior can erase an entire week of calorie deficit. Educate clients that rest days require the same nutritional discipline as training days.

Nutrition Myth Buster

The top nutrition myths your clients will bring to sessions — with the science and the trainer script to address each one.

FALSE

Eating after 8pm causes fat gain

Total daily calories determine fat gain or loss — not the time of day food is consumed. Nighttime eating is associated with weight gain primarily because people tend to overeat calorie-dense foods in the evening, not because of any metabolic clock.

"Your body processes food the same way at 9pm as it does at noon. What matters is total calories for the day. The issue with late-night eating is usually what people are eating — not when."
FALSE

Carbs make you fat

Excess calories make you fat — not carbohydrates specifically. Carbohydrates are the primary fuel for high-intensity training. Restricting carbs can impair performance and recovery. The issue is overconsumption of total calories, not carbohydrates as a macronutrient.

"Carbs are not the enemy — they are actually the fuel your muscles prefer during our training sessions. The key is the right amount and the right timing."
FALSE

You need to eat every 2-3 hours to keep your metabolism up

Meal frequency has minimal impact on metabolic rate. Total daily protein and calorie intake matter far more than how many meals they are spread across. Meal frequency should be based on personal preference, hunger signals, and lifestyle — not metabolism myths.

"Your metabolism is not a fire that goes out if you do not feed it every 2 hours. What matters is what you eat across the whole day — not how often."
FALSE

Fat makes you fat

Dietary fat is essential for hormone production (including testosterone and estrogen), cell membrane integrity, fat-soluble vitamin absorption, and joint health. Excess calories — from any macronutrient — lead to fat storage. Healthy fats from whole foods are a critical part of a performance diet.

"Dietary fat and body fat are not the same thing. Your body actually needs fat to produce hormones that help you build muscle. What makes you gain body fat is eating more calories than you burn."
NUANCED

Clean eating is enough without tracking

Eating whole foods is excellent for health and performance. However, even healthy foods contain calories and macronutrients. Many clients significantly overestimate how clean they eat and underestimate portion sizes. Tracking — even for 2-4 weeks — creates calorie and protein awareness that most clients lack.

"Clean eating is a great foundation. But even healthy foods have calories. I often suggest tracking for just 2 weeks — not forever — just so you know what you are actually eating. Most clients are surprised."
FALSE

Detox diets reset your metabolism

The liver and kidneys continuously detoxify the body — no diet can enhance or accelerate this process. Detox diets typically cause rapid water weight loss mistaken for fat loss, and often result in muscle catabolism due to very low protein intake. There is no scientific evidence supporting metabolic reset claims.

"Your liver and kidneys are already running the best detox program available 24 hours a day. Juice cleanses and detox teas cannot enhance that process — what they mostly cause is water loss, not fat loss."
NUANCED

You need protein within 30 minutes of training

The post-workout anabolic window is real but much wider than 30 minutes — approximately 4-6 hours total around the training session. Pre-workout protein extends this window further. Total daily protein intake is the primary driver of muscle protein synthesis. However, for GLP-1 clients and older adults where protein intake is already compromised, post-workout protein is still a valuable priority.

"The 30-minute window is not as strict as once thought. As long as you get protein within a few hours of training, you are fine. What matters most is hitting your total protein for the day."
FALSE

Protein is bad for your kidneys

High protein intake is safe for healthy individuals with normal kidney function. This myth originated from studies on individuals with pre-existing kidney disease — a completely different population. For healthy clients, protein intakes up to 2.5-3.0 g/kg have been shown to be safe in multiple long-term studies.

"That concern is specifically for people who already have kidney disease. For healthy people, the research is very clear that high protein diets are completely safe and actually beneficial."

Intermittent Fasting — Trainer Guide

What trainers need to know when clients ask about or are already practicing intermittent fasting. Stay within scope of practice — educate, do not prescribe.

Common Protocols

IF Methods Your Clients Use

  • 16:8 — 16-hour fast, 8-hour eating window (most common)
  • 18:6 — 18-hour fast, 6-hour eating window
  • 5:2 — 5 normal days, 2 days at 500-600 kcal

What the Research Says

Evidence Summary

  • IF works for weight loss primarily through calorie restriction — no metabolic magic
  • No significant advantage over continuous calorie restriction for fat loss
  • May improve insulin sensitivity in some populations
  • Protein distribution is compromised — harder to spread across 4-5 meals
  • Training fasted is fine for low-intensity only — NOT for strength or HIIT
Trainer Concerns

When IF is Problematic

  • Muscle preservation: Cramming protein into a short window makes hitting targets harder
  • GLP-1 clients: Already eating very little — IF further reduces intake
  • Older adults: Anabolic resistance + IF = poor MPS distribution
  • Disordered eating risk: IF can mask or trigger restrictive patterns
  • Training performance: Fasted training reduces strength and power output

When a Client Asks About IF

"Intermittent fasting can work for some people, but it works because of calorie reduction — not because of the fasting window itself. My concern for you is making sure we hit your protein target, which is harder in a shorter eating window. Alcohol and Body Composition

The science of how alcohol impacts fat loss, muscle building, recovery, and training performance. Critical knowledge for honest client conversations.

The Core Problem

Alcohol is 7 kcal/gram — With Zero Benefit

  • 7 kcal/gram — more calorie-dense than protein or carbs
  • Body prioritizes metabolizing alcohol over fat — fat oxidation stops
  • Lowers inhibitions leading to overeating (pizza at 2am phenomenon)
  • Mixed drinks can easily add 500-1000+ kcal in a single night
  • A "moderate" night out can erase an entire week of calorie deficit
Muscle and Recovery

How Alcohol Destroys Gains

  • Reduces muscle protein synthesis by up to 24% (even with adequate protein)
  • Suppresses testosterone for up to 72 hours
  • Disrupts sleep architecture — specifically REM and deep sleep
  • Impairs glycogen replenishment
  • Increases cortisol — promotes abdominal fat storage
  • Dehydration compounds all of the above

If a Client Drinks — Harm Reduction

  • Choose spirits with zero-calorie mixers over beer or cocktails
  • Set a drink limit before going out and stick to it
  • Eat protein before drinking (slows absorption, reduces overeating)
  • Alternate every drink with a glass of water
  • Do not train the day after heavy drinking — recovery is already impaired
  • Track alcohol calories just like food calories

🔬 Peptide Research & Education

Educational reference for certified trainers. Clients are increasingly asking about peptides — this tab gives you the scientific background to have informed, responsible conversations and know when to refer.

⚕️ Scope of Practice Disclaimer

Personal trainers cannot recommend, prescribe, or guide clients on peptide use. This information is strictly for trainer education — to understand what clients may be asking about and when to refer to a physician. All peptide decisions must be made under physician supervision. Most peptides listed here are not FDA-approved for human use and are classified as research chemicals.

DefinitionPeptides are short chains of amino acids (2–50) that act as signaling molecules throughout the body. Many naturally occurring hormones — including insulin and GLP-1 — are peptides. The fitness and wellness industry uses the term specifically for synthetic research peptides being explored for recovery, performance, and anti-aging. Why Clients Are Asking
  • Popularized by Joe Rogan, Andrew Huberman, and other high-profile figures
  • Wellness clinics and anti-aging centers are increasingly promoting peptide "stacks"
  • Online communities share anecdotal recovery results
  • RFK Jr. publicly discussed reducing FDA restrictions on peptides in 2025
  • Seen as a "natural" alternative to steroids or pharmaceuticals
The Reality
  • Most are not FDA-approved for human use — sold as "research chemicals"
  • Human clinical trial data is very limited — most evidence is animal models
  • Quality control is a significant concern — unregulated products
  • WADA bans many peptides in competitive sport
  • Your role: Educate, refer to physician, never recommend
What It Is15-amino-acid synthetic peptide derived from a protein naturally found in gastric juice. First isolated by researchers at University of Zagreb in the 1990s. Over 100 preclinical papers published since 1997. Proposed Mechanisms (Animal Studies)
  • Promotes angiogenesis — formation of new blood vessels to injured tissue
  • Modulates FAK-paxillin pathway — cell migration and wound closure
  • Anti-inflammatory activity — reduces inflammatory markers
  • May support collagen synthesis and connective tissue repair
  • Gastroprotective effects on GI mucosa
What Clients Claim
  • Faster tendon and ligament recovery
  • Reduced joint pain and inflammation
  • Improved gut health
  • General tissue repair acceleration
The Science Reality
  • All compelling evidence is preclinical (animal/cell models)
  • No published, peer-reviewed human clinical trials demonstrating efficacy
  • One unpublished Phase I trial (2015) — limited data
  • FDA has taken enforcement action against companies selling injectable BPC-157
  • WADA classifies as S0 Unapproved Substance — banned in sport
  • Cancer concern: FAK/paxillin activation pathways can also promote cancer cell migration — unresolved risk
Trainer Talking Points"I've heard of BPC-157, but as your trainer I cannot recommend any peptide therapy. If you are interested in exploring it, that conversation needs to happen with a physician who can review your full health history and monitor you appropriately."

What It IsSynthetic fragment of Thymosin Beta-4 (Tβ4), a naturally occurring protein present in virtually all mammalian cells. TB-500 contains the active region of the full protein. Proposed Mechanisms
  • Regulates actin polymerization — essential for cell motility and tissue repair
  • Facilitates migration of stem cells and reparative cells to injury sites
  • Promotes angiogenesis (new blood vessel formation)
  • Anti-inflammatory and wound healing properties in animal models
  • Often studied in combination with BPC-157 as complementary mechanisms
Commonly Combined WithBPC-157 ("Wolverine stack") — BPC-157 targets vascularization, TB-500 targets cellular migration. Complementary but no human data on combination. Regulatory Status
  • Not FDA-approved for human use
  • Banned by WADA in competitive sport
  • Sold as "research chemical" — quality control concerns

What They ArePeptides that stimulate the pituitary to release growth hormone (GH). Often marketed for muscle gain, fat loss, sleep improvement, and recovery. Frequently discussed in anti-aging circles. Key Compounds
  • CJC-1295: GHRH analogue — stimulates prolonged GH/IGF-1 elevation. Some human data showing GH increases in healthy adults.
  • Ipamorelin: Selective GHRP — pulsatile GH release with minimal cortisol/prolactin effects. Considered more targeted than older GHRPs.
  • MK-677 (Ibutamoren): Not technically a peptide — oral GH secretagogue. Has human clinical trial data; studied in older adults and GH-deficient populations.
Claimed Benefits
  • Improved deep sleep (GH is primarily released during slow-wave sleep)
  • Enhanced muscle protein synthesis and recovery
  • Improved body composition
  • Connective tissue support
Concerns
  • GH elevation carries cancer risk concerns — GH/IGF-1 axis promotes cell proliferation
  • Water retention, insulin resistance, carpal tunnel — known side effects of GH excess
  • Limited long-term human safety data
  • Most are banned by WADA

Training Calculators

Evidence-based calculators for training and nutrition. Use these tools to personalize programming for any client at any level.

Formulas Used

Epley: 1RM = Weight × (1 + Reps/30)  |  Brzycki: 1RM = Weight × 36/(37 − Reps)  |  Lombardi: 1RM = Weight × Reps^0.10  |  Average of all three shown as primary estimate.

1RM is an estimate — actual max may vary ±5–10%. Never test true 1RM without a spotter. Use estimated 1RM for programming percentages only.

Training Percentages

Karvonen Formula (Heart Rate Reserve Method)

Target HR = ((Max HR − Resting HR) × Intensity%) + Resting HR  |  Max HR = 220 − Age (or measured). More accurate than age-predicted alone as it accounts for individual fitness level.

Karvonen zones are more individualized than simple % of max HR. Resting HR measurement: sit quietly for 5 minutes, measure pulse for 60 seconds first thing in morning before getting up.

Formula

Base daily intake = Body weight (lbs) × 0.5 oz  |  Exercise add: +12 oz per 30 min of moderate exercise, +16 oz per 30 min of intense exercise  |  Heat add: +8–16 oz in hot/humid conditions.

These are general recommendations. Individual needs vary based on sweat rate, body composition, altitude, medications, and health conditions. GLP-1 clients and 55+ clients may need additional reminders — diminished thirst sensation is common. Always advise clients to monitor urine color — pale yellow is ideal.

Fat-Free Mass Index (FFMI)

FFMI = Fat-Free Mass (kg) / Height (m)²  |  Normalized FFMI = FFMI + 6.1 × (1.8 − height in m)  |  Natural upper limit for males ≈ 25–26. Bodybuilder context: 20–22 = good, 23–25 = excellent, 25+ = elite/enhanced. Females: 17–19 typical, 19–21 elite natural.

FFMI requires accurate body fat % measurement for meaningful results. Use alongside skinfold, DEXA, or BIA measurements. For bodybuilding clients, track FFMI over time to assess lean mass gain progress relative to total weight changes.

Powerlifting & Bodybuilding

Evidence-based training content, periodization models, certifications, and resources for coaching intermediate to elite strength and physique athletes. Content aligned with USAPL, USPA, NASM-PBC, and NSCA CSCS standards.

Periodization Models for Powerlifting
LINEAR

Linear Periodization (LP)

Simplest model. Increase weight each session or week. Best for beginners and early intermediate. Classic Starting Strength / StrongLifts approach.

  • Progress: +5 lbs/session upper, +10 lbs lower
  • Works until stalls — then switch models
  • 3×5 or 5×5 main lifts + accessories
  • Deload every 4–6 weeks or when stalled 3x
DUP

Daily Undulating Periodization

Vary rep ranges within the same week. More training variety, better for intermediate lifters who've exhausted LP. Evidence-based — strong research support.

  • Mon: Heavy (3–5 reps, 85–90% 1RM)
  • Wed: Moderate (6–8 reps, 75–80% 1RM)
  • Fri: Light/Volume (10–12 reps, 65–70% 1RM)
  • Rotate stimulus = sustained adaptation
BLOCK

Block Periodization

Sequenced training blocks targeting specific adaptations. Used by USAPL/IPF elite level programs. Popularized by Juggernaut Training Systems.

  • Accumulation (4–6 wks): Volume, 65–75% 1RM
  • Intensification (3–4 wks): Strength, 80–90% 1RM
  • Realization/Peak (2–3 wks): Competition prep, 90%+
  • Deload → repeat cycle
CONJUGATE

Conjugate / Westside Method

Concurrent development of multiple qualities. Max Effort and Dynamic Effort days. Popularized by Louie Simmons / Westside Barbell. Best for advanced powerlifters.

  • Max Effort (ME): Work to daily 1–3RM on variation
  • Dynamic Effort (DE): 50–60% 1RM, max speed (8–12×2-3)
  • 4 days/week: ME Upper, DE Upper, ME Lower, DE Lower
  • Rotate ME exercises every 1–3 weeks to prevent staleness

Meet Day — Attempt Selection Strategy
OPENERS

Opening Attempts

Opener should be a guaranteed white-light lift that you could hit on your worst day.

  • Squat: 90–92% of competition-day max
  • Bench: 90–92% — conservative is smart
  • Deadlift: 88–90% — fatigue factor is real
  • Rule: If it feels hard in warm-up, go lighter
ATTEMPTS 2 & 3

Second and Third Attempts

Second should be solid. Third is glory or a calculated push.

  • 2nd attempt: 97–100% of realistic max
  • 3rd attempt: PR attempt if 2nd felt good
  • 3rd attempt: Repeat 2nd if it was a grind
  • Willingness to leave weight on the platform = total preservation
WARM-UPS

Meet Day Warm-Up Protocol

Work backward from your opener. Allow 2–3 min between heavy warm-ups.

  • Bar × 2 sets × 5–8 reps
  • 50% × 1×5, 70% × 1×3, 80% × 1×2
  • 90% × 1×1, 95–100% of opener × 1×1
  • Time last warm-up to be 8–10 min before your attempt
RULES

USAPL / USPA Lift Commands

Know the commands before your client competes. Red lights for missed commands are avoidable.

  • Squat: "Squat" → descend → "Rack"
  • Bench: "Start" → press → "Rack"
  • Deadlift: No command until lockout → "Down"
  • USAPL is IPF-affiliated (drug tested). USPA has tested + untested divisions.

Hypertrophy Science — Key Principles (RP / Israetel Framework)
VOLUME

Volume Landmarks

Dr. Mike Israetel's evidence-based volume framework for each muscle group.

  • MEV (Min Effective Volume): Least sets needed to grow
  • MAV (Max Adaptive Volume): Optimal growth range
  • MRV (Max Recoverable Volume): Most sets before overtraining
  • Start at MEV, progress toward MAV over mesocycle
  • Typical MAV: Quads 16–20 sets/week, Back 16–22 sets/week
INTENSITY

Rep Ranges & RIR

Hypertrophy occurs across a wide rep range if taken close to failure. RIR (Reps in Reserve) is the primary intensity metric in modern bodybuilding.

  • 5–30 reps all effective if 0–3 RIR at set end
  • 6–12 reps: Practical sweet spot for most exercises
  • RIR 0–1: Best stimulus, hardest to recover from
  • RIR 2–3: Good stimulus, better long-term sustainability
  • Progress: Drop RIR across mesocycle (3→2→1→0)
FREQUENCY

Training Frequency

Most muscle groups respond better to 2x/week frequency vs. 1x/week for hypertrophy. More sessions = more stimulation opportunities.

  • 2×/week per muscle group: Evidence-based minimum
  • 3×/week possible for advanced athletes on high volume
  • Upper/Lower or Push/Pull/Legs: Both support 2× frequency
  • Bro split (1×/week) can work with very high volume per session
SELECTION

Exercise Selection Principles

NASM-PBC and RP framework for exercise selection in hypertrophy programming.

  • Choose exercises with full ROM and strong stretch position
  • Prioritize exercises with minimal joint pain for that client
  • Compound first (heavier, fresher), isolation last
  • Identify and address lagging muscle groups directly
  • Rotate exercises every mesocycle to prevent accommodation

Advanced Bodybuilding Techniques
TECHNIQUE

Rest-Pause Sets

Perform a set to near-failure. Rest 10–15 seconds (still holding position or in rack). Complete additional reps. Increases total reps and time near failure without adding sets.

  • Best for: Arms, shoulders, chest isolation
  • Example: DB curl to failure, 15s rest, 3–5 more reps
TECHNIQUE

Mechanical Drop Set

Change exercise mechanics (not weight) to extend a set past failure. Move from harder to easier variation of the same movement pattern.

  • Example: Incline curls (long head stretch) → flat curls → concentration curls, no rest
  • Exhausts the muscle progressively through mechanical advantage
TECHNIQUE

Blood Flow Restriction (BFR)

Partial occlusion of venous blood flow using a cuff or band. Allows hypertrophy stimulus at very low loads (20–30% 1RM). Used in post-rehab and for joint-sparing hypertrophy.

  • Cuff pressure: 60–80% limb occlusion pressure
  • Protocol: 4 sets (30-15-15-15 reps) with 30s rest
  • Contraindications: Cardiovascular conditions, DVT risk, open wounds
TECHNIQUE

Mind-Muscle Connection

Intentional focus on the target muscle during contraction. Research shows internal focus (thinking about the muscle) increases EMG activation vs. external focus (thinking about the weight moving).

  • Best applied during isolation exercises
  • Slower eccentrics (3–5s) enhance connection
  • "Feel the muscle, do not just move the weight"
TECHNIQUE

Lagging Body Part Specialization

Prioritize underdeveloped muscle groups by training them first in the session, increasing their frequency/volume while temporarily reducing other muscle group volume.

  • Add 1–2 additional sessions/week for lagging group
  • Reduce volume of well-developed areas during specialization block
  • 4–6 week specialization mesocycles
TECHNIQUE

Giant Sets

4+ exercises performed back-to-back with minimal rest. Extremely high metabolic stress. Best for smaller muscle groups or conditioning blocks in contest prep.

  • Example: Lateral raise → front raise → rear delt fly → upright row — 4 exercises, no rest between
  • High fatigue — program judiciously
Off-Season (Bulk) vs Pre-Contest (Cut) — Full Protocol
OFF-SEASON

Lean Bulk Protocol

Goal: Maximize muscle gain while minimizing fat accumulation.

  • Surplus: +200–350 kcal above TDEE
  • Protein: 1.6–2.2 g/kg BW
  • Carbs: 5–7 g/kg — maximize glycogen
  • Fat: 1.0–1.5 g/kg
  • Rate of gain: 0.5–1 lb/week (beginners), 0.25–0.5 lb/week (advanced)
  • Track weekly weigh-ins — adjust surplus if gaining too fast (>1 lb/week advanced = too much fat)
PRE-CONTEST

Contest Prep Cutting Protocol

Goal: Lose body fat while preserving maximum lean mass. Rate of loss determines lean mass retention.

  • Deficit: 300–500 kcal below TDEE
  • Rate of loss: 0.5–1% BW per week (faster = more muscle loss)
  • Protein: 2.3–3.1 g/kg — elevated during cut to protect muscle
  • Carbs: Reduce progressively as fat drops
  • Minimum: 12–16 weeks out for most athletes
  • Track body composition, not just scale weight
ADVANCED

Carb Cycling

Vary carbohydrate intake based on training demands. High carb on heavy training days, low carb on rest/cardio days. Maintains performance while in a deficit.

  • High carb day: Training + 25–50g above maintenance carbs
  • Moderate carb: Normal training sessions
  • Low carb: Rest days or cardio only
  • Protein stays HIGH on all days (2.3–3.1 g/kg)
ADVANCED

Refeeds & Diet Breaks

Temporary increases in calories during a deficit to mitigate metabolic adaptation, manage hunger, and improve adherence.

  • Refeed day: 1–2 days at maintenance calories (carb-focused)
  • Every 2–4 weeks during cut
  • Diet break: 1–2 weeks at maintenance mid-prep
  • Benefit: Restores leptin, reduces cortisol, improves psychological recovery
PEAK WEEK

Peak Week — Water & Carb Protocol

Final week manipulation to maximize stage conditioning. High individual variability — always practice in off-season before competition.

  • Mon–Wed: Deplete glycogen with low carb + high training volume
  • Thu–Sat: Carb load — 8–12 g/kg BW progressive load
  • Water: Maintain normal intake until 24–36hrs out
  • Sodium: Moderate manipulation — athlete-specific
  • ⚠️ Scope of practice: Refer to sports dietitian for individualized peak week protocols
RECOVERY

Reverse Dieting

Gradually increase calories post-contest to restore metabolic rate without excessive fat regain.

  • Add 50–100 kcal per week for 8–16 weeks
  • Prioritize carb increases over fat
  • Monitor body weight — aim for ≤1 lb/week gain
  • Avoid the post-show binge — metabolic rate is suppressed
